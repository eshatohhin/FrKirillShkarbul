During Great Lent, we face spiritual trials to test our true love for Christ and our willingness to follow Him to Golgotha. It’s crucial to understand the mystery of accepting Christ and recognizing the forces that opposed Him—like Judas, the religious leaders, and those inspired by Satan. They were monotheists, yet they rejected the true God.

Today, many claim all monotheistic faiths are similar, but this is deceptive. Even the Antichrist will mimic Christ, appearing good but embodying falsehood. This is the danger: a fake Christian, who outwardly copies faith but serves a different spirit, is more dangerous than an open pagan or atheist. We see this in groups like the Masons, who use belief in “God” to lead people away from truth.

The key is distinguishing between a true image of Christ—reflecting His love, sacrifice, and reality—and a mere copy that only mimics externally. Ancient Israelites fell into idolatry not by abandoning monotheism, but by filling it with wrong content—worshipping a god of their own desires, like the golden calf or Baal. Similarly, cults like Mithraism imitated Christian rituals but served Satan.

This pattern repeats in history: the Jewish religious establishment of Christ’s time, though building a grand temple, ended up opposing God. Later, Catholicism and Protestantism often retained Christian forms but sometimes lost the true spirit, leading to worldly power struggles and apostasy. Even in Orthodoxy, the danger exists—adhering to tradition without loving Christ leads to empty formalism or ecumenism, both paths away from grace.

Our safeguard is genuine love for Christ. Without it, we risk slipping into worshipping the Antichrist, deceived by external similarities. We must be true icons of Christ, not copies. As we face coming challenges, let us seek humility, self-sacrifice, and true devotion, resisting the deceptive spirits that seek to lead us astray. May God guide us through Lent and beyond to inherit His salvation. Amen.
