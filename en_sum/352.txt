As we approach Passion Week, I want to reflect on the mystery of death and resurrection, especially in a world filled with war and suffering. Many ask why God allows such things—whether it’s punishment, human politics, or the work of Satan. But today, I invite you to look deeper into the relationship between death and life in God’s plan.

Scripture shows us that God sometimes permits death—as with the firstborn in Egypt, the Assyrians at Jerusalem, or Ananias in the New Testament—not out of cruelty, but as a form of judgment to prevent greater evil and awaken His people. Death, in these instances, serves a divine purpose: it halts the spread of sin and calls us back to truth.

Yet, as followers of Christ, we are called to embrace a paradox: we take up our cross—a symbol of death—to gain true life. We hate sin, which brings death, but we love the cross, through which we die to our old selves and live for Christ. This means following Jesus means facing suffering, persecution, and even death, just as He did. He warned His disciples, "They hated me, they will hate you." Our path is not easy, but it is the way to resurrection life.

In today’s wars, we see Satan’s hand—aiming to drive people into despair and away from God. Yet, God also allows these trials to purify His people, to stop sin, and to draw a faithful remnant closer to Him. Through suffering, some lose faith, but others discover that only in Christ can we find meaning and strength.

Our task is not to blame or hide from death, but to face it with courage, trusting in God’s ultimate sovereignty. We must live in constant dialogue with Christ, taking up our cross daily, showing compassion to all who suffer, and standing firm in faith, love, and humility. In doing so, we participate in His death and resurrection, finding joy even in pain, as true disciples awaiting the fullness of life in Him.
